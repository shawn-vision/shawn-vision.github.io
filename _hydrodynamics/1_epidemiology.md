### Why mobility matters in epidemics

Most epidemic models emphasize <strong>local infection dynamics</strong>â€”how fast infection spreads within a community.
But real outbreaks unfold across space, driven by <strong>movement</strong> between communities.

<div style="
  background: #F1FAFF;
  border-left: 6px solid #0055A4;
  padding: 14px 18px;
  border-radius: 12px;

  color:#0F172A;
">
Even when most people stay put, a <strong>small fraction</strong> of traveling infected individuals can dominate
<strong>front speeds</strong> and generate <strong>organized spatial patterns</strong> (waves, hotspots) far from the origin.
</div>

In our work, we call these highly mobile infected individuals <strong>geographical spreaders</strong>. Their role is
analogous to â€œfast transport pathwaysâ€ in fluid systems: rare, but disproportionately influential.

### What this framework enables

- **Quantifying how mobility controls outbreak speed** (front propagation)  
- **Identifying when small mobile subpopulations dominate spread**  
- **Explaining waves/hotspots as transport-driven pattern formation**  
- **Guiding interventions** that target movement pathways, not only transmission rates
