### Why wall friction is a fundamental turbulence problem

A central challenge in fluid mechanics is explaining <strong>energy dissipation in turbulent flows</strong>
arising from friction at boundaries. This ‚Äúwall friction‚Äù problem directly controls pressure drop and
transport efficiency in pipelines and channels‚Äîcritical for moving oil, methane, and water across continents.

<div style="
  background: #F1FAFF;
  border-left: 6px solid #0055A4;
  padding: 14px 18px;
  border-radius: 12px;
  margin: 18px 0;
  color:#0F172A;
">
Classical practice relies on empirical <strong>Nikuradse/Moody diagrams</strong> linking friction factor to
<strong>Reynolds number</strong> and <strong>relative roughness</strong>. Our goal is to replace curve-fitting with a
<strong>first-principles mechanism</strong> grounded in turbulence theory.
</div>

### What this framework enables

- **First-principles prediction of friction factor** from turbulence spectra  
- **A unified view of Nikuradse/Moody behavior** across regimes  
- **Mechanistic interpretation of roughness transitions** and critical points  
- **Improved transport modeling** for pipelines, channels, and engineered networks
